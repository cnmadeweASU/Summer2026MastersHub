Engaging Diverse Learners in Secondary Science: A Research Synthesis and Content Vision

Chasity N. Madewell · SED 510 — General Methods · SLO 5: Justifying Perspectives and Decisions Through Credible Research

Introduction and Position

I teach seventh-grade life science at American Leadership Academy, where a single class period holds a genuinely wide range of learners: multilingual students, students who read well below grade level, students with ADHD, gifted students who finish early, and the focus student from this course's classroom profile, whom I refer to as "Mateo," who reasons brilliantly out loud but struggles with dense text. The subtopic I have explored most persistently in this course is how to engage all of those learners in real science rather than a watered-down version of it. The perspective I want to test against the research is one I hold strongly but have not always been able to defend: that engagement for diverse learners is not a matter of personality, charisma, or making science "fun," but a matter of deliberate instructional design—access, language, student voice, and a compelling phenomenon built into a unit from the first planning decision. To examine that claim, and following the synthesis process modeled in this module's foundational resources, I curated five peer-reviewed sources published within the last five years, each approaching the question from a distinct angle, and read them for where they converge, where they complicate one another, and what they ask of me as a designer of science instruction.

Access Built In, Not Bolted On

Almeqdad, Alodat, Alquraan, Mohaidat, and Al-Makhzoomy (2023) synthesized the literature on Universal Design for Learning in a systematic review and meta-analysis, concluding that designing instruction around multiple means of engagement, representation, and action and expression measurably improves access and participation for academically diverse learners. The study contributes a quantitative, cross-study warrant for building options into a lesson from the outset rather than retrofitting accommodations afterward. It supports my perspective that access is a design decision, while cautioning that the size of the benefit depends heavily on implementation quality—a reminder that UDL is a discipline to practice, not a label to claim.

Planning for Variability: Differentiation as Decision-Making

Jager, Denessen, Cillessen, and Meijer (2025) examined what secondary teachers actually do when they differentiate and how they reason about those choices, finding that responsive differentiation—adjusting for students' readiness, interests, and learning profiles—remains uncommon despite decades of advocacy. The article contributes a sober, practice-level view that the central obstacle is teacher decision-making, not a shortage of named strategies. It both supports and complicates my perspective: differentiation clearly matters, yet the research shows it is difficult and rare, which tells me I must plan it deliberately into a unit in advance rather than trust it to improvise itself in the moment.

Language and Culture as Resources for Sensemaking

Grapin, Ramos Borrego, and Navarro (2025) reviewed translanguaging research in U.S. K–12 science and engineering education through an equity lens, showing that multilingual students' full linguistic repertoires are resources for scientific sensemaking rather than obstacles to it. The review contributes an asset-based reframing of a population too often defined by deficit, while critically noting that researchers conceptualize "equity" itself in inconsistent ways across the literature. It strongly supports the equity instinct I carried from social work into teaching, and it reshapes my vision: my multilingual learners' home languages become tools for figuring out science, not barriers to be remediated before science is allowed to begin.

Engagement as a Designed, Situational Condition

Bjerga Pettersen, Vaaland, Ertesvåg, and Virtanen (2024) studied secondary students' situational engagement and found that it rises when teachers show regard for adolescents' perspectives, keep lessons productive, and choose active instructional formats over passive ones. The study contributes momentary, classroom-level evidence that engagement is something teachers structure rather than a fixed trait students either possess or lack. It directly supports my perspective that engagement is designed, not inborn. Because the data are situational and correlational, I read the findings as strong design guidance—respect student voice and build in active participation—rather than as proof of a single guaranteed cause of engagement.

Phenomena as the Vehicle for Equitable Science

Walker and Nouri (2025) conducted a PRISMA systematic review of twenty-four studies on phenomenon-based learning and storylines in K–12 science, concluding that anchoring instruction in real-world phenomena shows genuine promise for engagement and scientific reasoning but depends on sustained professional development, deliberate scaffolding, and culturally responsive design. The review contributes a science-specific vehicle for what the other sources argue in general terms. It supports my vision of phenomenon-anchored units while honestly flagging that the approach can fail diverse learners when scaffolding is thin—precisely the gap that my UDL and differentiation planning are meant to close before a unit ever reaches students.

Synthesis: What the Evidence Says Together

Read together, these five sources converge on a single claim with striking consistency: engaging diverse learners in secondary science is a problem solved in the design phase, not rescued in the moment. Almeqdad et al. (2023) establish that access should be planned as multiple pathways from the start; Jager et al. (2025) show that the same logic, applied as differentiation, is widely endorsed but rarely enacted, locating the gap in teachers' planning and reasoning; Grapin et al. (2025) and Walker and Nouri (2025) move the conversation into science specifically, arguing that multilingual repertoires and real-world phenomena are the assets and anchors through which diverse students actually do science; and Bjerga Pettersen et al. (2024) supply the moment-to-moment evidence that engagement follows from respecting student voice and choosing active formats. An emerging trend across the newest work is the shift from a deficit frame to an asset frame—from asking how to remediate students before they can learn science to asking how their languages, cultures, and curiosity become the means of learning it.

A credible synthesis also has to weigh the quality of its evidence. Three of these sources are systematic reviews or meta-analyses (Almeqdad et al., 2023; Grapin et al., 2025; Walker & Nouri, 2025), which provide breadth and reduce the bias of any single study, but which also aggregate work with heterogeneous designs and inconsistent definitions—Grapin et al. explicitly document that researchers define "equity" in conflicting ways, and Walker and Nouri report that the phenomenon-based studies they reviewed frequently lacked strong scaffolding and aligned assessment. The differentiation research (Jager et al., 2025) is descriptive and reveals a persistent research-to-practice gap rather than a proven intervention, and the engagement findings (Bjerga Pettersen et al., 2024) are situational and correlational rather than causal. The honest conclusion is that the evidence is convergent in direction but uneven in rigor. That is exactly why I hold these findings as design principles to implement and then test against my own classroom data rather than as guarantees—a stance that runs through all of my SLO work this term.

Content Vision Statement

My vision for content in secondary science is that every learner in the room can do authentic, grade-level science when the unit is engineered for human variability from the first decision, rather than narrowed to an imagined average student and patched afterward. Concretely, that means anchoring each unit in a compelling phenomenon worth explaining (Walker & Nouri, 2025); designing multiple means of engagement, representation, and expression so that access is the default rather than the exception (Almeqdad et al., 2023); planning—not improvising—differentiation for the specific readiness, interests, and profiles of my learners (Jager et al., 2025); treating my multilingual students' full linguistic repertoires as instruments for sensemaking rather than obstacles to it (Grapin et al., 2025); and structuring every lesson around student voice and active participation so that engagement is built rather than hoped for (Bjerga Pettersen et al., 2024). Throughout, the science target stays rigorous and constant; what flexes is the path to it. This is the same conviction that moved me from social work into the classroom—that the students most often left at the margins are precisely the ones a well-designed system can reach—now grounded in current research and carried directly into the "Cells: The Building Blocks of Life" unit I have built across this course. Engagement, in this vision, is not the reward for good content; it is the evidence that the content was designed for everyone.
