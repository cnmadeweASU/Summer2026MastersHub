So… what is a "digital citizen"?

Here's the honest answer: nobody fully agrees. If you search "digital citizenship" online, you'll find a hundred different definitions from teachers, tech companies, and governments. That's okay — it's a big idea, and it's still growing as the internet changes.

But underneath the arguments, almost everyone is really talking about the same thing: being a person online the same way you'd want to be a person in real life — safe, smart, honest, and kind. The internet isn't a different planet with different rules. It's just a bigger room with the door propped open. This playbook gives you six moves that work in that room.

The Six Moves

1
Think Before You Trust

Don't believe everything the screen tells you.

  • Ask "who made this, and why?" before you share. Ads, jokes, and AI-made images can all look real.
  • Read sideways. Open a new tab and check what other sites say about the source — not just what the source says about itself.
  • Slow down on stuff that makes you angry. Strong feelings are exactly what fake or misleading posts are built to trigger.
News Literacy Project Stanford: Check It Online
2
Your Digital Footprint

The internet remembers — like, forever-ish.

  • "Delete" doesn't always mean gone. Screenshots, reposts, and backups can outlive the post you erased.
  • Future you is watching. Coaches, colleges, and bosses really do search names. Post like the grown-up you'll become is in the room.
  • Build a footprint you're proud of — projects, art, kindness. A good trail is a head start, not just a risk.
Common Sense: Digital Citizenship
3
Digital Etiquette

Be the kind of person you'd want in your comments.

  • Type it only if you'd say it to their face — with a teacher standing right there.
  • Don't be a bystander. If someone's being piled on, don't join, don't share it. Check on them, and tell a trusted adult.
  • Assume you read it wrong first. Text has no tone. Ask before you fire back.
StopBullying.gov
4
Safety & Security

Lock the doors before you decorate the house.

  • Strong, different passwords — a short phrase beats one tricky word. Never hand them out, not even to a "friend."
  • Keep private stuff private: full name, address, school, and location off public profiles.
  • If something feels off, tell an adult. Strangers who rush you or ask for secrets are the warning sign — not your fault, always reportable.
Be Internet Awesome NetSmartz
5
Digital Wellness

Protect your headspace.

  • Phones away at bedtime. Screens steal sleep, and tired brains can't learn, play, or chill. Charge it outside your room.
  • Highlight reels aren't real life. People post their best 1%. Comparing your whole self to it isn't a fair fight.
  • Notice how an app leaves you feeling. If it makes you anxious or mean, that's data. Take a break — you're allowed to log off.
Family Media Plan (AAP)
6
Data & Privacy

If it's free, you might be the product.

  • Apps collect a lot — your location, contacts, what you watch and for how long. That info is worth money to them.
  • Check the permissions. A flashlight app doesn't need your photos. Turn off what an app doesn't truly need.
  • You have a say. Privacy settings, "limit tracking," and saying no to over-sharing are real tools. Use them.
FTC: Online Privacy
