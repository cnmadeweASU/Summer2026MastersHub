Designed for Everyone: A Content Vision for Seventh-Grade Life Science

Chasity N. Madewell · SED 510 — General Methods · SLO 6: Creating an Equitable and Inclusive Learning Environment

Content Relevance and the Question of Who Does Science

Content relevance, in my classroom, is not a question of whether seventh-graders find cells interesting. It is a question of who gets to do real science. I teach seventh-grade life science at American Leadership Academy, and a single class period holds multilingual students, students reading well below grade level, students with ADHD, gifted students who finish in half the time, and the focus student from my classroom profile, whom I call “Mateo,” who reasons brilliantly out loud but stalls on dense text. The question a content area has to answer for that room is not “is this engaging?” but “is every one of these learners actually doing the discipline, or only the few the lesson was quietly built for?” I came to teaching from social work, as a Licensed Master of Social Work who believed the classroom was the most hands-on place to put real tools in young people’s hands. That conviction is the root of my content vision: the students most often handed a watered-down version of a subject are precisely the ones a well-designed unit can reach with the real thing.

The Five-Day Unit as a Ladder to the Summative

Across this course I built a three-week anchor unit, “Cells: The Building Blocks of Life,” and planned its first five instructional days (August 3–7, 2026) as a deliberate ladder toward one summative performance: a Claim–Evidence–Reasoning explanation of how a cell’s structure determines its function, paired with the authentic “Design-a-Cell” analogy model students build and present. Every day’s objective is a rung on that ladder. On Day 1, students observe their own cheek cells and onion skin under the microscope and meet the cell theory; the objective—providing evidence that living things are made of cells (NGSS MS-LS1-1)—gives them the foundational evidence the summative claim will later rest on. Day 2 asks them to compare prokaryotic and eukaryotic, plant and animal cells through a card sort and Venn analysis, building the precise vocabulary of structural difference the final explanation depends on. Day 3 is the conceptual heart of the unit: using a “Cell City” analogy and an organelle jigsaw, students describe how each organelle’s structure serves its function (MS-LS1-2)—the exact relationship the summative asks them to argue, and the analogy that seeds their model. Day 4 widens the lens from one cell to the levels of biological organization, where students model how specialized cells build tissues, organs, and systems (MS-LS1-3), so the closing argument can travel from a single organelle all the way up to a body system. Day 5 is the summative itself: students write the CER and present their analogy model to an authentic audience, demonstrating the one transferable idea the unit was engineered to deliver—that structure determines function at every level of life. Because a Day 1 diagnostic sets my flexible groups and a daily exit ticket tells me who needs reteaching before the next rung, the ladder is not a fixed march; it bends to the students climbing it.

From Research Synthesis to Daily Design

That synthesis does not sit beside this unit; it is built into it. Reading five recent studies for where they converge changed how I plan, and each finding has a fingerprint in the calendar. Almeqdad and colleagues’ (2023) meta-analysis on Universal Design for Learning convinced me to make access a default rather than a retrofit, which is why the draw-and-label microscope task on Day 1, the picture-based card sort on Day 2, and the option to submit the Day 5 explanation as a recorded oral response all let students show science thinking without a reading bottleneck—for Mateo, his oral reasoning and diagramming become the entry point rather than the workaround. Jager and colleagues (2025) found that differentiation is widely endorsed yet rarely enacted because the obstacle is teacher decision-making in the moment, which is exactly why I planned every flexible group, sentence frame, and gifted extension into the calendar in advance instead of trusting myself to improvise them while teaching. Grapin and colleagues (2025) reframed multilingual students’ home languages as instruments for sensemaking, so the Spanish cognates (célula, tejido, órgano), bilingual glossaries, and bilingual labeling on the final model are tools for figuring out science, not translation favors. Bjerga Pettersen and colleagues (2024) showed that engagement rises when teachers respect adolescents’ perspectives and choose active formats, which is why student choice anchors the Day 5 model and why every day turns on a microscope, a sort, a jigsaw, or a build rather than a lecture. And Walker and Nouri (2025) confirmed that phenomenon-based science genuinely engages diverse learners but fails them when scaffolding is thin—so the unit opens on a real phenomenon, “these are your own living cells,” and then scaffolds deliberately and thickly, closing the very gap their review warns about.

A Designer’s Stance: Values, Evidence, and Leadership

What the synthesis ultimately reshaped is not a single lesson but my stance toward the work. Helping build STRATOSYN moved me from surviving the day-to-day toward seeing curriculum as a system I design, and the research gave that instinct an evidence base. This is my fifth year in education and the first time I will teach the same content twice, which means I am no longer assembling a unit to get through a week—I am refining a system I can document, measure, and hand to another teacher. It is also why I hold these findings as design principles to implement and then test against my own engagement and achievement data rather than as guarantees: three of my five sources are systematic reviews whose evidence is convergent in direction but uneven in rigor, and the honest move is to build on them and then verify in my own room. An equitable, inclusive content vision, in this sense, is not a belief I assert. It is a system I build, measure, and improve—which is exactly the work I am preparing to lead.

Content Vision Statement

My vision for content in secondary science is simple to state and demanding to practice: every learner in the room does authentic, grade-level science because the unit was engineered for human variability from its first design decision, not narrowed to an imagined average student and patched afterward. In practice that means anchoring each unit in a phenomenon worth explaining (Walker & Nouri, 2025); designing multiple means of engagement, representation, and expression so access is the default (Almeqdad et al., 2023; CAST, 2018); planning differentiation in advance for the real readiness, interests, and language profiles of my students (Jager et al., 2025; Tomlinson, 2014); treating multilingual repertoires as instruments for sensemaking rather than obstacles to it (Grapin et al., 2025); and structuring every lesson around student voice and active participation so engagement is built rather than hoped for (Bjerga Pettersen et al., 2024). Throughout, the science target stays rigorous and constant; only the path to it flexes. The “Cells” unit is the first full expression of this vision, and its implication for my practice is concrete: I will teach it, measure who it reached, and refine it—and carry the same engineering discipline into every unit and, in time, into the teachers I help lead. An engaged classroom, in this vision, is not evidence that the lesson was fun; it is evidence that the content was designed for everyone in the room.
